# Trusted Floors Under Untrusted Learners: A Runtime Assured-SLO Guard for ML Serving

# Abstract 

Modern ML serving increasingly lets learned, unbounded 

components (routers, latency-SLO admitters, admit ladders) decide a tenant’s quality of service; when one is wrong, the assured SLO can silently break, and the Kubernetes layers beneath (Kueue, DRA, the Gateway-API Inference Extension, GAIE) only add cross-layer surprises. Rather than trust the learner to be right, we bound the damage a wrong one can do : a small trusted guard wraps the untrusted learner— learned proposes, the guard disposes . We observe that a tenant’s assured-SLO obligation splits into two parts with different epistemics. Its safety projection (per-request dispatch feasibility and a per-class, per-window service floor) is a controllable obligation a small guard enforces at runtime .The guard holds it regardless of an arbitrarily-wrong learned admitter , by reservation and priority dispatch, with dropping infeasible work a safety net. Its guarantees are conditional ,scoped by strength: the admission floor and drop rules hold 

by construction , the service floor modulo backend capacity, the worst-case sojourn only in a characterized envelope under the stated assumptions (§2). Its aggregate obligation (a tail-latency percentile over the workload ensemble) is a statistical residual with no per-request enforcement point; it can only be conservatively screened , and a cheap deterministic ( 𝑝 50 )screen is optimistically unsound not only near saturation (𝜌 → 1) but, we measure, well below it. We build the guard (a Simplex-style assured-floor gate plus assured-first priority dispatch) and show on real 2×V100 that it holds assured-class miss 0.0 (admitted-basis; reps 10 , worst upper Wilson CI 0.0053 ) across every miscalibration of a learned admitter under two shed policies that fail in opposite directions (overload, and rejecting assured), with priority dispatch shown load-bearing. Against the strongest 2026 baseline (a real ,deployed GAIE Flow Control on a serving simulator), a mislabelling router flips the same assured requests from miss 

0.0 to 1.0; our guard instead reserves by the true class, so the label cannot break its floor. We characterize when acheap config screen can be trusted (in overload away from the knee, where a 𝑝 50 accept is already sound; a conservative percentile extends this) and when it cannot (near saturation, the tightest deadlines, and, for a 𝑝 50 screen, even below saturation). Assurance is thus a property of the guard’s reservation +priority floor (given its preconditions), not of the learned component, which is hard to evaluate traditionally since one cannot enumerate all wrong learners. As a Frontiers 

submission we evaluate the stance on commodity 2×V100 and a serving simulator, and scope datacenter scale, real-model Flow Control, and a closed worst-case theorem as the agenda. 

# 1 Composition without assurance 

A tenant’s quality of service is no longer set in one place. Mod-ern ML-serving stacks increasingly let learned, unbounded 

components (smart routers, latency-SLO admitters, admit lad-ders) decide it at runtime. That QoS is itself composed across four independently-configured layers: cluster quota and pri-ority (Kueue’s ClusterQueue /WorkloadPriorityClass ), GPU device claims (DRA, resource.k8s.io ), inference-gateway priority and (experimental) Flow-Control admis-sion (GAIE’s InferenceObjective ), and runtime scheduler knobs. When one component is wrong, the tenant’s assured floor can silently collapse. Three facts make this dangerous. 

(i) Cross-layer surprises are real: we catalogue eight hazards where the composed semantics surprise the operator (e.g. a DRA alias double-counts a device; a Kueue quotaCheck-Strategy flag alone flips admission of the same workload; App. A). (ii) Existing tools miss them: kubectl --dry-run 

and single-object OPA validate one object at a time and cannot compose layers. (iii) The new mechanisms are learned and unbounded: GAIE’s latency-SLO admitter, learned routers, and admit ladders “guarantee” SLOs only empirically, and under FIFO they cannot protect the assured class even when well-calibrated .The field’s response has been to predict better . We take a deliberately conservative stance. This paper’s central idea: 

bound the damage a wrong learned component can do, rather than trust it to be right . A small trusted guard wraps the untrusted learner ( learned proposes, the guard disposes )and holds each tenant’s floor by its true contract, however the learner errs; and we are precise about which part of an SLO this stance even makes sense for. 

Two obligations, two epistemics. The idea rests on a split: part of an assured SLO is a safety property a runtime guard can 1arXiv:2607.09992v1 [cs.DC] 10 Jul 2026 enforce request-by-request, and part is a statistical property it can at best screen . Formally, model executions as timed event traces; let ˆ𝑠 (𝑅 ) be a service estimate the system carries for request 𝑅 , and fix a window 𝑊 and a per-window assured budget 𝐵 . A tenant’s assured SLO bundles: (a) a safety projection —DR , “no request is dispatched when it can no longer meet its deadline” ( 𝑡 + ˆ𝑠 (𝑅 ) > 𝐷 (𝑅 )); AF , “in every window of length 𝑊 the assured class is granted ≥ 𝐵 tokens from its reserved bucket”; and SF , “in every window ≥ 𝐵 

assured requests complete by deadline.” Each has a finite, irremediable bad prefix, so each is a safety property [ 2 , 11 ]; and (b) an aggregate obligation— P95 , “the 95 th-percentile assured sojourn time over the arrival/service ensemble ≤ 𝐷 ”— a statistical predicate on the trace ensemble, in the SLA class of hyperproperties [7], with no finite bad prefix. These demand different treatment. The safety projection is runtime-enforceable : not by Schneider truncation (which only halts) [ 23 ] but as an edit/suppression automaton [ 15 ]: a serving guard suppresses requests and reorders dispatch live, with controllable admission in the sense of Basin et al. [ 3]. DR and the admission floor AF are enforceable unconditionally ;the service floor SF is enforceable modulo capacity ≥ 𝐵 /𝑊 .DR implies on-time completion only under the soundness side-condition ˆ𝑠 ≥ 𝑠 . The aggregate P95, by contrast, is neither per-trace enforceable nor soundly provable by a deterministic first-moment model as 𝜌 → 1: the sojourn-time percentile grows like (1 − 𝜌 )−1 driven by service-time variance (Pollaczek– Khinchine) [ 12 , 13 ], which such a model omits. Enforce the safety projection; screen the statistical residual : the guard that enforces the first (§2) and a characterization of when a cheap static screen can—and cannot—be trusted for the second (§3). 

Early evidence the stance is promising. On real 2×V100 hardware the guard holds the assured floor (miss ≈0, reps 10 )around an arbitrarily-miscalibrated learned admitter, where the unguarded stack misses 0.86 –0.94 . And against the strongest 2026 baseline, a real , deployed GAIE Flow Control (EPP v 1.5.0, flow-control enabled), we measure that Flow Control dispatches by the router’s declared priority, so a mislabelling router flips the same assured requests from miss 

0.0 to 1.0; our guard reserves by the true class, immune to the label by mechanism—a cross-reference to the V100 guard runs, not a same-stack head-to-head. The learner’s damage is bounded where the leading learned-routing stack’s is not. 

Contributions—a Frontiers submission. (1) The stance :a trusted floor that bounds an untrusted learner’s damage, made precise by the safety-vs-statistics epistemic split that says which part of an SLO it covers. (2) A guard realizing it, with real-hardware evidence that it holds where an unguarded stack, a well-calibrated FIFO admitter, and live GAIE Flow Control do not. (3) An honest characterization of when a cheap static screen can be trusted for the residual, including a held-out result that it is a characterization , not a certifier. We evaluate on commodity 2×V100 and a serving simulator; datacenter scale, real-model Flow Control, and a closed worst-case theorem are the aspects that are hard to evaluate, scoped as the agenda (§5). 

# 2 The guard: learned proposes, veri-fied disposes 

The guard’s mechanism is classical real-time/QoS schedul-ing: a CBS-style bandwidth reservation [ 1 ] the opportunistic class can never draw from, + assured-first non-preemptive fixed-priority dispatch, + a firm-deadline drop (deferred-red), wrapping the untrusted learned admitter/router. Protecting a reserved class regardless of a misbehaving lower-priority load is the defining guarantee of reservation and mixed-criticality 

scheduling [ 21 , 28 ]; we borrow the stance of runtime assur-ance (Simplex: hold “regardless of the outputs of the advanced controller” [ 20 , 22 , 24 ]) but not its machinery—there is no decision module and no verified-fallback switch. Our claim is not the scheduler but its target : the reserved object is the safety projection {DR, AF, SF |capacity} of §1, and the untrusted component is an arbitrarily-wrong learned serving admitter—so the assured floor is protected by reservation 

(AF) and priority (SF), while deferred-red (DR) would keep a doomed request off the server past its deadline (an unexercised limb here; see below). 

What the “true class” is—the trust boundary. The reser-vation keys on the tenant’s contract class. That class, we assume, is fixed at the gateway from the request’s authenti-cated tenant identity (namespace/ServiceAccount, the way Kubernetes already binds tenancy), before any learned compo-nent runs; the untrusted router/admitter sits downstream and only proposes —route, priority hint, admit/reject—so it can misdirect or drop a request but cannot rewrite the class key its floor is drawn from. This is precisely the boundary GAIE Flow Control lacks: FC dispatches by the InferenceObjective 

priority the routing layer sets , so a mislabel crosses straight into dispatch (our label-swap: the same assured requests miss 

0.0 → 1.0); our floor is keyed one layer earlier. The edge is therefore conditional on that binding: absent an authenticated class, no reservation—ours or FC’s—can tell a mislabel from the truth, so binding it end-to-end is a deployment assumption we state but do not close.

# 3 When can a cheap static screen be trusted? 

The guard (§2) protects a live request; the ecosystem will nonetheless want to catch cross-layer misconfigurations before 

they ship, with a cheap static check. This section asks a subor-dinate question— where can such a check be trusted —and an-swers it empirically. We provide a conservative offline screen: each layer modeled as a transfer function pinned to the shipped component by trace-equivalence conformance, composed in a discrete-event simulator that returns a counterexample trace or declines. It is not a prover but a conservative check (abstract interpretation [ 8]) with characterized unsoundness [ 18 ]. Two honest facts scope it. First, it is two disjoint analyses : a timing simulator (below) and a separate quota-conservation arithmetic (the double-count/silent-skip hazards); the timing screen catches no config hazards, and the quota screen is not hardware-validated. Second, its timing verdict is fed a 

measured service estimate, so its accuracy figures are post-dictive. A deployable use needs a pre-deployment ˆ𝑠 with the side-condition ˆ𝑠 ≥ 𝑠 , obtainable from a short batch-1-to-𝐶 

calibration probe (batch-1 ≈236 ms; batch-𝐶 ≈590 ms me-dian, 2×V100) run once before the workload. Feeding a high batch-𝐶 percentile (padded to the ceiling 𝑠 max ≈ 660 ms) gives the conservative ˆ𝑠 ≥ 𝑠 the screen needs, at some completeness cost (over-estimating the typical service). The residual is the batch-coupled under-load service (§2), which the probe 

bounds but does not predict .

# 5 An agenda: from a by-construction floor to a proved one 

Scope: what we can and cannot evaluate. As a Frontiers submission we are explicit about the evaluation’s reach. We do 

evaluate, on real commodity hardware: the guard’s floor under adversarially-miscalibrated learners (reps 10 , 2×V100); live 

GAIE Flow Control’s label-trust and floorlessness (a real EPP v1.5.0 deployment); and the static screen’s soundness over 

1320 hardware cells plus a held-out calibration. Three things are hard to evaluate and we do not claim them. (i) Datacenter scale : our testbed is a single commodity 2×V100 node, so multi-node scale-out and datacenter accelerators are untested. 

(ii) Real-model Flow Control end-to-end : we exercise FC’s queueing mechanism on the official vLLM simulator (which isolates exactly the priority-vs-reservation contrast at issue) rather than a production model server, and a head-to-head with the guard on one stack is future work. (iii) A closed worst-case theorem : continuous batching couples service time to load (§2, A3), so the load-independence a clean bound needs does not hold in general; only the 𝐵 =𝐶 corner is worst-case certified. The demonstrated result is thus a structure ; the four problems below turn it into a guarantee. 

Discharge the capacity precondition. SF holds only for backend capacity ≥ 𝐵 /𝑊 (A4), and no guard conjures service. That precondition is itself a composed-config fact (DRA placement, batch width, MoE expert availability), the cross-layer property the static screen (§3) should monitor. Apreliminary run bears this out: a batch cap cutting throughput 

62% leaves the naive fixed floor at 3/120 on time vs a lowered guard floor’s all-it-serves (full-class miss 0.833 vs 0.975 ); the screen proved the degraded floor before the run, and the open mechanism makes it a guard that watches its assumption online. 

Close the worst-case guard theorem. The §2 sketch bounds assured sojourn by a reserved-ahead term plus a non-preemptible tail 𝑠 opp max ; bounding it worst-case (not worst-observed) needs a stochastic service model (continuous batch-ing makes service load-dependent). This theorem converts the 7empirical 0.0 into a proof; it is serving-specific and the paper’s sharpest open problem. It is also output-length-parametrized: the measured batch-𝐶 service grows ≈183 + 14 .9 ℓ ms with output ℓ (the clean same-batch median at ℓ ∈ { 64 , 128 , 256 };§3’s ≈590 ms is the batch-𝐶 probe under sustained load, 

∼170 ms higher as batching couples service to load), so a 

256 -token workload needs 𝐷 ≳ 8 s: the cap bounds 𝑠 max at every length, but the floor’s deadline grows with the output. 

Make the screen sound near 𝜌 → 1. The 𝑝 99 knob is an empirical patch, not a proof: a sound near-saturation screen needs a probabilistic model checker or a proven headroom margin 𝜌 ≤ 1 − 𝜖 trading admitted load for soundness. 

Survive a moving upstream. Whether trace-equivalence conformance holds as the ecosystem absorbs a mechanism every one–two releases is an empirical question about the upstream, not the theory. Extending the guard to verified in-flight mode transitions under DRA capacity loss is ongoing: a preliminary real-DeviceTaintRule run load-shed gracefully, safety resting on a conservative degrade action .

# 6 Conclusion 

An assured SLO is not one obligation but two: a controllable safety projection a trusted guard enforces around a wrong learned admitter, and a statistical residual a conservative screen can only approach . Separating them lets a small trusted floor bound an untrusted learner’s damage: on real hardware the guard holds the assured class ( admitted-basis miss 0.0 ) where an unguarded stack, a well-calibrated FIFO admitter, and 

live GAIE Flow Control (which dispatches by the router’s label) do not; and the cheap screen is honest about where only the guard survives (the queueing knee, and for a 𝑝 50 

screen sub-saturation too). This is a Frontiers contribution: the stance and its early evidence are here; datacenter scale, real-model Flow Control, and a proved worst-case floor are the agenda we invite the community to close.
