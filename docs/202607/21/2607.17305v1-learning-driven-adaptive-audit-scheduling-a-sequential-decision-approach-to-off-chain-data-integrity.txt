Abstract. We model cryptographic auditing of off-chain data as a Con-strained MDP (CMDP) under partial observability : the storage node’s hidden type and corruption state make the problem a POMDP, while a miss-rate ceiling ¯ρ imposes an explicit security constraint. We propose 

DRQN-CMDP , a Deep Recurrent Q-Network whose GRU layer main-tains a belief over the latent node type, paired with Lagrangian dual ascent that adapts the miss-rate penalty λ automatically. A pairing-free homomorphic-MAC primitive supplies O(1) on-chain verification cost. Across 13 methods—four DQN variants, PPO, A2C, PPO-Lagrangian, a stateful Bayesian heuristic, three fixed-rule baselines, and an oracle-informed heuristic. DRQN-CMDP achieves a favourable balance: ∼83% 

lower gas than fixed high-frequency auditing, single-digit miss rate (7.5% ), and moderate detection latency—a combination no other method matches across all three objectives simultaneously. 

Keywords: Constrained MDP · Deep recurrent Q-network · Partial observabil-ity · Provable data possession · Adaptive scheduling 

# 1 Introduction 

Outsourcing bulk data to off-chain layers (content-addressed networks, edge caches) is standard when on-chain storage is impractical. The verifier must still obtain evidence that remote replicas remain intact without re-downloading the

full corpus. Provable Data Possession (PDP) and proofs of retrievability supply such evidence, yet two structural difficulties persist. First, verification must be economically viable when checks are anchored on a public ledger: pairing-heavy PDP instantiations map poorly to smart-contract gas budgets. Second, when and how thoroughly to audit is itself a control prob-lem: aggressive schedules waste budget on mostly benign intervals, while lax schedules leave data exposed to undetected tampering. These objectives form an 

impossible triangle —no static policy simultaneously minimises cost, miss rate, and detection latency under drifting node behaviour. 

Problem statement. We formulate adaptive audit scheduling as a Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP) under partial observability. The node’s true type (honest or malicious) and exact corruption count are hidden, making the problem a POMDP. At each decision epoch the controller chooses an audit in-terval ∆t and a sampling ratio p, observes noisy feedback (pass/fail, latency, reputation), and seeks to minimise expected cumulative verification cost while keeping the miss rate Rmiss ≤ ¯ρ. We enforce this constraint via Lagrangian dual ascent on a learnable penalty λ: not a hard guarantee, but stable empirical constraint tracking that removes the need for manual reward tuning. 

Why fixed and heuristic rules fail. Corruption is non-stationary : malicious nodes may accelerate tampering after long quiet periods; honest nodes exhibit rare faults clustered in time. Fixed intervals ignore state entirely. Rule-based thresh-old policies react to coarse predicates but cannot optimise long-horizon trade-offs between premature audits (high cost, low information gain) and delayed audits (longer undetected exposure). Both are myopic with respect to the latent belief over hidden node type and future corruption intensity. 

Contributions. (i) CMDP formulation of cryptographic audit scheduling with a learnable miss-rate constraint—the first such formulation for repeated PDP veri-fication. (ii) DRQN-CMDP : a GRU-augmented Dueling Double Q-Network with primal-dual λ updates that convert a hard-to-tune penalty into a self-regulating mechanism. A pairing-free MAC supplies O(1) on-chain verification, ground-ing the gas cost term in realistic economics. (iii) Broad evaluation against 12 baselines (DQN family, PPO, A2C, PPO-Lagrangian, Bayesian heuristic, fixed-rule, oracle-informed) with ablation, sensitivity, and constraint-sweep analyses on matched seeds. 

# 2 Related Work 

PDP/POR and compact proofs. PDP [1] and POR [2] established remote in-tegrity checking without full download. BLS-style constructions [3] yield short proofs but rely on pairings, ill-suited for on-chain gas budgets. Dynamic vari-ants [4] and privacy-preserving extensions [5] retain expensive primitives. Cru-cially, none addresses when to invoke audits as a learned policy. Learning-Driven Adaptive Audit Scheduling 3

Blockchain-anchored auditing. Recent systems integrate auditing with smart contracts [6,7], emphasising proof correctness and contract logic but assuming exogenous schedules or one-shot challenges. Our focus is the configuration of repeated audits as the object of sequential optimisation. 

Constrained and safe RL. Constrained MDPs formalise safety or budget limits as inequality constraints on expected costs [15]. Lagrangian relaxation converts con-straints into adaptive penalty terms, enabling standard policy solvers [14,16]. We adopt this primal-dual mechanism for miss-rate enforcement and demonstrate that it outperforms fixed-penalty shaping in our domain. 

Recurrent RL for partial observability. When the full state is hidden, recurrent architectures such as DRQN [17] maintain a belief via LSTM or GRU layers. Our setting—where the node’s latent type and corruption count are unobservable— is a natural POMDP; the GRU in DRQN-CMDP serves precisely this belief-tracking role. 

Positioning. We are the first, to our knowledge, to combine constrained and recurrent RL for cryptographic audit configuration under partial observability with explicit security–cost–delay trade-offs.

# 5 Industrial Architecture and Workflow 

The proposed scheduler is intended for a control plane surrounding an existing storage and smart-contract system; it does not place neural network inference on chain. This separation keeps the trusted contract small and makes policy updates possible without migrating stored data or redeploying verification logic. 6 C. Lin et al. 

System components. The data plane comprises the storage node, its tagged block store, and the audit contract. The control plane contains four services: (i) a telemetry collector that normalises contract events, response latency, and rep-utation signals; (ii) a policy service that maintains the GRU hidden state and selects (∆t, p ); (iii) a challenge generator that expands a fresh seed into sampled indices and coefficients; and (iv) an evidence recorder that binds the chosen ac-tion, proof result, transaction identifier, and policy version into an auditable log. Only the challenge and compact proof cross the data-plane boundary. 

Online audit lifecycle. One decision cycle proceeds as follows. 1. The collector forms ot from the most recent contract and network events and attaches the current policy version. 2. The DRQN-CMDP policy updates its recurrent state and selects an interval and sampling ratio. Operational bounds may clip either component before execution. 3. At the scheduled time, the challenge generator emits a fresh seed. The storage node reconstructs the sample and returns (σ, μ ) before a fixed timeout. 4. The contract verifies the aggregate and emits a pass, fail, or timeout event. A fail can trigger replication repair, quarantine, or a higher-assurance follow-up audit. 5. The event becomes the next observation and is appended to the replay and compliance logs. During training, episode-level statistics also update λ.

Operational safeguards. Learning-based scheduling should be enclosed by deter-ministic safety rails. A production deployment should impose a minimum and maximum audit interval, cap p to protect the prover from overload, and re-serve a fixed high-frequency action as a fail-safe. The fail-safe should be selected when telemetry is missing, the recurrent state is unavailable, or recent observa-tions fall outside the training envelope. After a model update, a shadow period can compare new and incumbent actions without letting the new policy control transactions. These mechanisms separate availability risk from model quality and make rollback an operational action rather than a retraining task. 

Policy versioning and accountability. Every decision should record a model hash, observation timestamp, selected action, any safety override, and the resulting contract event. Such records allow an operator to reconstruct why an audit was delayed or intensified and to compare realised miss rate with the declared ceiling 

¯ρ. This is particularly important in an industrial setting, where the learned policy changes transaction frequency and therefore affects both security exposure and cost.

6.2 Architecture and Training 

DRQN-CMDP maps observations through a feature extractor (two 128-unit ReLU layers), a GRU recurrent layer (hidden size 64), and Dueling heads —value (scalar) and advantage ( |A| ) streams recombined as Q = V + A − A [10]. The GRU maintains a belief vector ht over successive decision steps. Because the agent never directly observes the node’s true type, ht implicitly tracks latent corruption dynamics from the observation history—an approach motivated by DRQN [17] for POMDPs, without requiring explicit Bayesian filtering. Complete episodes are stored in an episode replay buffer. At each learning step the agent samples a batch of length-T sub-sequences ( T =8 by default), ini-tialises the GRU hidden state to zero, and back-propagates through the unrolled network. Double Q-learning [9] provides the TD targets; a masked MSE loss ignores zero-padded positions in shorter episodes.

Design rationale. Why value-based over policy-gradient? Actions are discrete and low-dimensional ( |A| =25 ); rewards are sparse and heavy-tailed (large negative spikes on misses). Off-policy replay is sample-efficient under these conditions; Section 8 confirms that DRQN-CMDP outperforms PPO and A2C baselines. 

Why recurrence? The hidden node type induces partial observability; without a belief mechanism the agent cannot distinguish a quiet malicious node from an honest one. 

6.4 Training and Online Execution 

Training separates fast per-transition value updates from the slower episode-level constraint update. Within an episode, sequence replay preserves the temporal information needed by the GRU. At the episode boundary, the pooled miss statistic updates λ once, avoiding high-variance dual changes after individual audits. Table 1 summarises the procedure. The separation between training and execution is operationally useful. On-line inference requires only one GRU forward pass and no gradient computation. Retraining can therefore run asynchronously on logged episodes, while the ac-tive version continues serving decisions. A new checkpoint should be promoted only after offline constraint evaluation and a shadow comparison against the incumbent policy.

# 10 Conclusion 

We formulated off-chain audit scheduling as a constrained POMDP and proposed DRQN-CMDP: a recurrent value-based agent whose GRU maintains a belief over the hidden node type, with a Lagrangian dual-ascent loop that converts the miss-rate ceiling ¯ρ into a self-regulating penalty λ. A pairing-free MAC supplies O(1) 

on-chain verification, grounding the cost signal in realistic economics. Across 13 methods, DRQN-CMDP uniquely combines low gas (8.4), single-digit miss rate (7.5%), and moderate latency (9.1)— a three-way balance no other method matches. Ablation confirms that recurrence and adaptive λ are complementary; constraint sweeps show ¯ρ as a single operator knob for navigating the security– cost frontier.
