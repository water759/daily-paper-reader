# Information-Theoretically Secure Aggregation for Lightweight Federated Learning: Resilient to Dropouts and Adversaries 

Hyeong-Gun Joo, Member, IEEE, Songnam Hong, Senior Member, IEEE, and Dong-Joon Shin, Senior Member, IEEE, 

Abstract —On-device federated learning (FL) enables privacy-preserving and personalized model training on resource-constrained devices such as smartphones and IoT nodes. To reduce communication cost, sign-based methods (e.g., SIGN SGD) transmit one-bit gradients. However, exposing gradient signs makes them vulnerable to inference attacks, while existing secure aggregation schemes are often incompatible with such methods or incur significant computational and communication overhead. We propose a lightweight and information-theoretically secure aggregation framework tailored for sign-based FL. The framework securely computes the majority vote (MV) polynomial through single-round secure multiplication, ensuring end-to-end information-theoretic security under the honest-majority assumption while revealing only the final aggregated sign to the server. To enhance efficiency and scalability, we introduce two key techniques. First, inverse-form exponent reduction halves the effective MV polynomial degree, reducing both communication and computation costs. Second, we propose single-round secure multiplication, achieving linear offline complexity and storage with only a single online communication. Together, these tech-niques reduce online communication by up to 99.5% and latency by up to 85.7% compared to conventional approaches. Also, by leveraging inherent MDS-code-based decoding, the framework achieves robustness against both dropouts and adversarial be-haviors, yielding accuracy gains of up to 20.65% and 10.74%, respectively. Overall, the proposed framework establishes apractical foundation for large-scale, low-latency, and information-theoretically secure aggregation in sign-based FL. 

Index Terms —Dropouts and adversaries, information-theoretically secure aggregation, majority vote polynomial, sign-based federated learning, single-round multiplication. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The proliferation of intelligent mobile devices and the Inter-net of Things (IoT) has accelerated the adoption of on-device federated learning (FL)—a decentralized machine learning paradigm that enables privacy-preserving model training di-rectly on user devices [1]–[12]. By training locally without exposing raw data, FL addresses privacy concerns and is particularly attractive for resource-constrained environments such as smartphones, wearables, and edge networks. Despite its advantages, communication efficiency remains a critical bottleneck in FL, as participating devices often operate under stringent bandwidth and computation constraints [13]. To alleviate this issue, gradient compression techniques such as SIGN SGD have been proposed, where model updates

are quantized into compact one-bit representations [14]–[19]. While this approach significantly reduces communication cost, it also exposes individual gradient signs, making sign-based methods inherently vulnerable to inference attacks and privacy leakage [20], [21]. Recent studies demonstrate that gradient signs can be exploited for gradient inversion and membership inference attacks [21], [22], highlighting the urgent need for end-to-end privacy protection even under extreme 1-bit quantization. Existing secure aggregation schemes have attempted to mitigate these vulnerabilities, but significant limitations re-main. Approaches based on homomorphic encryption intro-duce prohibitive computational and communication overheads, rendering them unsuitable for resource-constrained edge or IoT environments. In addition, protocols that rely on pre-processed correlated randomness—such as Beaver triples or other MPC-style offline preprocessing [23]—incur substantial precomputation, storage, and communication costs, which further increase protocol complexity and limit practicality on lightweight devices [24]. Furthermore, most conventional secure aggregation frameworks are inherently incompatible with sign-based methods due to their structural mismatch. In particular, prior schemes assume linear aggregation over multi-bit gradients, whereas sign-based methods require a secure nonlinear majority voting operation which cannot be efficiently supported by conventional frameworks. A recent work, Hi-SAFE [25], presents the first secure ag-gregation framework compatible with SIGN SGD with majority vote ( SIGN SGD-MV), providing only computational security under cryptographic hardness assumptions while maintaining simple server-side aggregation and low latency through hierar-chical subgrouping. However, it still depends on preprocessed Beaver triples, incurring offline overhead, and its additive secret sharing makes it vulnerable to user dropouts and ad-versarial behaviors—an important limitation in practical FL settings. To overcome these challenges, we focus on information-theoretic security , which guarantees privacy even against ad-versaries with unbounded computational power [26]. Unlike computational approaches, it is entirely independent of any computational hardness assumptions or heavy cryptographic mechanisms such as homomorphic encryption or correlated randomness. This unconditional protection relies solely on randomness and the algebraic properties of secret sharing, making it particularly suitable for lightweight and long-lived arXiv:2607.20890v1 [cs.LG] 23 Jul 2026 2

Building upon this foundation, we present a lightweight and information-theoretically secure aggregation framework

This structural insight enables a fundamentally more efficient secure multiplication approach. We propose two complementary single-round secure mul-tiplication methods. Method A (DN + Classical N -BTE) handles general N -input multiplication using an subset-mask table, suitable for small-scale settings. Method B (Single-Mask Power Sharing) exploits the single-base structure of MV polynomials: by employing a single random mask and the binomial expansion, offline computational complexity and storage are reduced from exponential to linear while requiring only a single online opening. Furthermore, by leveraging inherent MDS-code-based de-coding properties of the secure multiplication, the proposed framework attains strong robustness against both user dropouts and adversarial corruptions, improving practical FL accu-racy by up to 20.65% and 10.74% under dropout and adversarial conditions , respectively. A. Contributions 

The key contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:  

> •

Lightweight and information-theoretically secure ag-gregation. We propose a lightweight secure aggregation framework that ensures information-theoretic privacy un-der the honest-majority assumption, without relying on any computational hardness assumptions or heavy cryp-tographic mechanisms. It remains secure against compu-tationally unbounded adversaries, making it suitable for long-lived, resource-constrained FL deployments.  

> •

End-to-end privacy via secure MV polynomial evalua-tion. The framework securely evaluates MV polynomials for sign-based FL, revealing only the final aggregated sign to the server and preventing inference from intermediate gradient signs.  

> •

Inverse-form exponent reduction. We introduce an al-gebraic transformation that rewrites high-degree terms as inverse powers, halving the maximum polynomial degree and further reducing both communication and computational costs.  

> •

Single-round secure multiplication. Using a single mask and the binomial theorem, it achieves single-round latency , linear offline computational complexity and storage, with minimal online communication —only a single field element per user.  

> •

Robustness against both dropouts and adversarial behaviors. By exploiting the inherent MDS-code prop-erties of secure multiplication, the proposed framework achieves robustness against both user dropouts and ma-licious participants, achieving accuracy improvements of up to 20.65% and 10.74%, respectively.

B. ATLAS Multiplication 

ATLAS [40] is an information-theoretically secure MPC protocol that improves both communication and round com-plexities under the honest-majority assumption ( t < n/ 2), where t is the maximum number of colluding or corrupted parties. While DN method [26] is simple and robust, its round and communication costs limit scalability for deep multiplication chains. ATLAS introduces two orthogonal optimizations:  

> •

Communication reduction. By using t-wise independent randomness, only t random double sharings need to be uniformly random, while the rest are derived via a hyper-invertible matrix. This amortizes the preprocessing cost and reduces total communication cost.  

> •

Round reduction. By forcing an output of DN multiplication into a Beaver-triple–friendly form, ATLAS allows Beaver triples for the next layer to be prepared in parallel. As a result, two consecutive layers of parallel multiplications can be evaluated in one reconstruction round, halving the number of rounds.

C. System Assumptions and Problem Scope 

We consider an FL environment under the following as-sumptions:  

> •

Semi-Honest Model: All users follow the protocol cor-rectly, but up to t of them may collude to infer private information from the exchanged messages [23], [43]–[48].  

> •SIGN

SGD-MV Update Rule: Each of n users transmits a 1-bit sign of its local gradient, and the server computes the majority vote over the received signs to determine the update direction [14]–[18].  

> •

Honest-Majority Setting: Information-theoretic security requires an honest majority, i.e., t < n/ 2. This assump-tion naturally aligns with SIGN SGD-MV, which relies on the majority of users providing correct local signs; if t ≥ n/ 2, the global majority vote itself becomes incorrect. Thus, the honest-majority setting is both a natural and essential condition for ensuring correctness and privacy. These assumptions provide the foundation for our proposed secure aggregation framework. In the following section, we present two complementary methods that achieve information-theoretic security under these assumptions while minimizing online communication costs and latency. V. P ROPOSED SECURE AGGREGATION METHODS 

This section presents two complementary components that together enable efficient and information-theoretically secure evaluation of MV polynomials in FL, ensuring end-to-end privacy by revealing only the final aggregated sign to the server. Unlike prior approaches that rely solely on binary Beaver triples or DN-style multiplication [26], [38], [40], the proposed framework introduces new primitives that jointly achieve substantial reduction in communication cost and la-tency , while keeping preprocessing and storage manageable. (i) Inverse-Form Exponent Reduction. We introduce an inverse-form polynomial construction that halves the max-imum exponentiation depth . This transformation reduces the number and depth of secure multiplications required to evaluate the MV polynomial, leading to lower communica-tion and computational costs without affecting accuracy or security. The reduced degree also enables scalable, single-round secure polynomial evaluation. (ii) Single-Round Secure Multiplication. To realize one-round secure multiplications, we propose two realizations, Method A and Method B, under a unified DN-based archi-tecture, each targeting different multiplication structures:  

> •

Method A (DN + N -BTE). This method adopts a two-layer structure: Layer 1 performs DN-style masked open-ings for binary multiplications, and Layer 2 aggregates the results using the classical N-BTE, enabling secure computation of the product of all pairwise multiplica-tions within one round under an honest-majority setting. However, it requires an exponential subset-mask table 

of size 2N −N −1, resulting in Θ( N · 2N ) offline DN multiplications and O(2 N ) storage. While Method A minimizes online latency, its scalability is limited by the exponential offline cost.  

> •

Method B (Single-Mask Power Sharing). For MV polynomial evaluation, Method B exploits the special structure where all multiplications involve powers of a single aggregated value xk rather than products of distinct inputs. Using a single mask a and the bino-mial theorem, Method B achieves linear O(N ′) of-fline computation and storage, where N ′ = ( p − 1) /2

is the maximum polynomial degree after inverse-form exponent reduction. This approach preserves the single-round online phase and honest-majority security while dramatically reducing overhead compared to Method A. Next, a secure aggregation framework is proposed, which combines (i) inverse-form exponent reduction for lower mul-tiplicative depth and (ii) single-round secure multiplication tailored to MV polynomial evaluation. Together, they achieve efficient, one-round, and information-theoretically secure ag-gregation suitable for large-scale federated learning. 

A. Inverse-Form Exponent Reduction of MV Polynomials 

MV polynomials recently arise in secure aggregation proto-cols for FL [25]. A representative construction, as in Eq. (2), involves high-degree monomials over a prime field Fp, result-ing in significant overhead for secure exponentiation. C. Simulation-Based End-to-End Security 

We analyze the end-to-end security of the proposed secure multiplication methods under the simulation-based paradigm, following the standard real/ideal execution framework in the semi-honest model with honest majority.

VIII. C ONCLUSION 

This paper presented a lightweight and information-theoretically secure aggregation framework for sign-based FL on resource-constrained devices. Under the honest-majority assumption, the framework securely computes MV polyno-mials via single-round secure multiplication, revealing only the final aggregated sign without intermediate leakage. By leveraging MDS-code-based error correction, it achieves ro-bustness against both user dropouts and adversarial behaviors. To enhance efficiency and scalability, we introduced two key techniques. First, inverse-form exponent reduction halves the effective MV polynomial degree, reducing both communica-tion and computation costs. Second, we proposed single-round secure multiplication methods, where the structure-aware method exploits the single-base property of MV polynomials to achieve linear offline complexity and storage with only a single online communication. Together, these techniques re-duce online communication by up to 99.5% and latency by up to 85.7% compared to conventional approaches. Experiments confirm that the framework maintains full accuracy under up to 35.7% dropout and 23.9% adversarial rates. Overall, the proposed framework establishes a practical foundation for large-scale, low-latency, and information-theoretically secure aggregation in sign-based FL. Future work will explore opti-mized implementations for heterogeneous edge environments and extensions to broader secure collaborative learning tasks.
