Abstract 

As LLM agents evolve from single-user assistants into shared organizational infrastructure, new privacy risks emerge: inappropriate information may not only be exposed through outputs for external recipients, but also internally across users through inter-agent messages, shared memory and agents. These data spillage risks are not captured by existing privacy benchmarks grounded in contextual integrity (CI) as they focus primarily on either single-user settings or interactions be-tween independently owned agents. We introduce 

PiSAs (Privacy in Shared Agentic systems), a benchmark for assessing unintentional leaks with dual CI annotations: whether an information is appropriate for the task, and which users may legitimately access it. This enables direct measure-ment of cross-user spillage across agentic system components and interfaces, such as outputs, inter-agent communication, and memory. PiSAs 

is system-agnostic and supports evaluation across different agent topologies and memory regimes. We find that, although system design improves CI compliance, results are bottlenecked by incorrect LLM judgment calls: even state-of-the-art models fail to reliably filter inappropriate content or restrict transmission to authorized users. Our find-ings underscore the need for privacy-preserving strategies, beyond those studied in this work. 

1 Introduction 

Large language model (LLM) agents are currently de-ployed to complete tasks on behalf of individual users. Extending their deployment to organizational settings, where agents coordinate across users rather than operating in isolation, has the potential to tap into col-lective intelligence for effectively automatizing tasks such as scheduling, resource allocation, performance reviews, and knowledge sharing. It also introduces considerable privacy challenges. A multi-user agentic system inevitably handles information that is appropriate in one context but not in another, creating opportunities for it to surface where it does not belong or spill to the wrong user. For instance, a team member’s medical appointment may be appropriately shared with HR but should not be surfaced when a colleague ask their availability for a meeting. The privacy risks introduced by a multi-user system are qualitatively different from those studied in single-users settings (Mireshghallah et al., 2025; Bagdasarian et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Zharmagambetov et al., 2025; Yagoubi et al., 2026). When one or multiple agents solve a task for one user, the concern is whether the output reveals sensitive information to the external recipient. When agents collaborate on behalf of multiple users, the risks multiply: sensitive attributes about one employee may propagate through inter-agent communication and become accessible to other employees who should not have access; personal data may accumulate in a shared memory store readable by all users regardless of organizational access rights; the final output may incorporate inappropriate information about individuals who never consented to have their information reach the recipient. Crucially, these failures are unintentional, i.e. they might arise from the ordinary mechanics of collaboration, and not from adversarial behaviors. These are violations of contextual integrity (CI): the principle that information flows appropriately when they match the norms of the social context in which the information was originally shared (Nissenbaum, 2004). Under CI, privacy is not a property of data in isolation but of flows: the same attribute can be appropriate to share in one task context and a violation in another, depending on who the sender, recipient, and subject are, and what norms govern the relationship between them. Unlike recent multi-user CI benchmarks (Juneja et al., 2025; Park et al., 2026) that consider systems of independently owned agents, we study a setting where multiple users share the same agentic system, where data spillage can happen not only in dialogues, but also through shared infras-tructure, such as agents and memory (Hu et al., 2026). We present PiSAs (Privacy In Shared Agentic 1

systems), a benchmark for systematic evaluation of unintentional privacy leakage in multi-user organi-zational settings. Each scenario in PiSAs involves a team of users and data subjects, each associated with a typed set of attributes carrying two orthogonal privacy annotations grounded in CI theory: a task-contextual appropriateness label , whether the attribute should be used to complete the task given the organizational context, and a per-person visibility structure , which users are privy to the attribute at all based on their role and past interactions. These dual annotations enable evaluation of two qualitatively distinct failure modes: inappropriate disclosure when gathering information to solve a task, and data spillage through agent-to-agent communication or shared memory. Unlike recent literature (Juneja et al., 2025; Wang and Jiang, 2026; Zou et al., 2026), PiSAs is system-agnostic: it is designed to support evaluation of virtu-ally any system configuration (e.g., number and type of agents, type of memory, type of communications). In our experiments, we evaluate three agentic system topologies: a single agent with access to all data, a centralized multi-agent system in which all commu-nication routes through a coordinator, and a decen-tralized peer-to-peer system. We further study three memory regimes: private per-agent memory, shared memory, and hybrid. Privacy is assessed at three levels: the output level (what appears in the final response), the communication level (attributes flow-ing through agent-to-agent communication), and the 

memory level (attributes persisting in memory stores beyond their intended visibility scope). Configura-tions are intentionally kept simple: exhaustive study or optimization of system designs are left for future work. 

Our main contributions are: • PiSAs, a new benchmark of 85 manually curated scenarios for evaluating unintentional privacy leak-age in multi-user agentic systems, grounded in dual CI annotations: task-contextual appropriateness labels and per-person visibility constraints, making cross-user spillage directly measurable. • A systematic study of system designs , spanning agent topology, memory configuration, prompt-level privacy instructions, and LLM backbones from three different families, isolating how each axis governs the privacy–utility trade-off. • An empirical evaluation at three leakage sur-faces , revealing that all system designs suffer from high violation rates: data and agent partitioning reduce leakage but do not eliminate it, adding memory improves appropriateness compliance but at the cost of higher cross-user spillage, and agents struggle the most with task-relevant attributes and when task decision rules are implicit. 

2 Related Work 

In Table 1 we report a comparison of privacy bench-marks in the literature, highlighting the principal characteristics of the studied instances/scenarios in the dataset and of the evaluated agentic systems. 

Single-user. Early works grounded in Contextual Integrity (CI) (Nissenbaum, 2004) have established principled evaluation of whether LLMs handle infor-mation flows appropriately. ConfAIde (Mireshghallah et al., 2024) probes LLMs with targeted questions about privacy norms in conversational settings, while GoldCoin (Fan et al., 2024) creates synthetic court case scenarios to evaluate legal privacy reasoning. CI-Bench (Cheng et al., 2024) and PrivaCI-Bench (Li et al., 2025a) extend this to form-filling and small agentic tasks. PrivacyLens (Shao et al., 2024) goes further by evaluating privacy norm awareness in agent trajectories, the first action-based benchmark with API calls in this space, but without information accu-mulation across turns. Two benchmarks specifically study how persistent context introduces new leakage surfaces: CIMemories (Mireshghallah et al., 2025) examines how privacy violations compound as a single user’s attributes inappropriately surface across different tasks when information accumulates in the LLM’s context; PersistBench (Pulipaka et al., 2026) studies cross-task leakage due to persistent memory but also memory-induced sycophancy, where stored preferences reinforce user biases. AgentDAM (Zhar-magambetov et al., 2025) evaluates data minimization in realistic web-navigation agents with API calls, framing privacy preservation as sharing the strictly necessary information. AgentLeak (Yagoubi et al., 2026) specifically studies multi-agent systems, with memory, API calls, and information accumulating across coordination turns. Finally, CI-Work (Fu et al., 2026) grounds evaluation in enterprise workflows with dense retrieval and API calls using a single ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) agent, but without accumulation nor spillage across tasks or users. As shown in Table 1, none of these benchmarks feature multi-user scenarios or measure cross-user spillage. 

Multi-user. MAGPIE (Juneja et al., 2025) is the first work to study privacy in multi-user settings. 3 PiSAs Benchmark 

Problem setting We study contextual-integrity (CI) in shared agentic systems where a team of users, with assigned professional roles, collaborates to complete a task. One user, that we dub the executor , is respon-sible for producing the final output for a recipient; the remaining users are peers that may hold task-relevant information (see Figure 1). Because the system spans the whole team (potentially through shared agents and shared memory) any piece of information (attribute) shared by one user with the system might become available to other users. This creates two violation types we wish to characterize: the system may use an attribute inappropriately when solving the task for the executor, or it may spill it to users for whom it was not intended. We are interested in quantifying CI viola-tions that are unintentional, arising from the system’s own reasoning rather than from user collusion or adversarial manipulation. For that, we craft scenarios where users are cooperative and without conflicting goals, and where in principle there is no privacy-utility trade-off: there exists a valid solution that does not require revealing inappropriate information. 

Evaluation Each scenario contains a set of attributes A, i.e., facts about a user or an entity, that are provided to a system as-is. For evaluation purposes, attributes are partitioned into a disjoint 

appropriate subset Aapp (information required to complete the task) and an inappropriate subset 

Ainapp (information that should not be used to complete the task). Each attribute is additionally annotated with a visibility matrix : the subset of users that have access to it, for instance because they participated in a conversation where that attribute was revealed, or could legitimately have access to it in the future, because of their role within the team. These annotations enable quantification of two types of violations: An appropriateness violation occurs when an inappropriate attribute surfaces in the task outcome or in inter-agent communications. A visibility violation occurs when any attribute, appropriate or not, reaches a user who should not have access to it (e.g., in the memory or communications of that user’s agents). We quantify both violation types via the metrics defined in §D, following Mireshghallah et al. (2025). We also measure completeness (the ratio of appropriate attributes gathered to complete the task) and utility (whether a task is correctly completed). 

Scenario Construction Unlike prior benchmarks, we cover only three task types within a single domain, trading breadth for depth. Our scenarios are complex in ways that LLM-generated benchmarks cannot capture: task-relevant information is distributed across users based on who would realistically hold it; each scenario is grounded in concrete organizational roles within a realistic team structure; tasks require genuine decision-making rather than form-filling; and arriving at a correct decision requires gathering and integrating information through multi-hop reasoning and communication. To instantiate realistic scenarios, each family of tasks comes with a structured seed that provides the task description, the possible outputs, the rules that de-termine if a task is solved correctly, the users and their professional roles, and the attributes populating the scenario. Each PiSAs scenario is built backward from a hidden oracle solution for the task. More precisely, in JIRA A LLOCATION the oracle solution is the optimal assignment of tickets to users, based on the appropriate attributes, which are tickets’ requirements, and users’ availabilities and specialties (e.g., backend, QA). The seed also provides inappropriate attributes, such as personal circumstances, medical context, performance context, or interpersonal conflict. In MEETING ALLOCATION , the oracle solution is the unique assignment of meetings to time slots and rooms, determined by factors like attendee availability, room requirements, deadlines, etc. The seed also includes inappropriate scheduling-adjacent attributes, such as sensitive personal reasons, soft preferences, or irrelevant private circumstances. In S EVERITY 

CLASSIFICATION , the seed defines an incident, a severity rubric for judging incident severity (among four possible levels), and role-specific evidence slots. Appropriate attributes are then the technical findings used by the rubric (e.g., whether customer-data access is confirmed, whether active exploitation is present), and inappropriate ones are non-evidentiary context such as customer pressure, audit concerns, team politics, or misleading benign explanations. Inappropriate attributes can arise in different ways. Some appear relevant because they create organi-zational pressure or suggest a plausible alternative 4interpretation, but they are not legitimate inputs to the task rule: for example, incident severity should not be determined by executive pressure or customer impor-tance, but only by the technical rubric. Others contain personal details that should not be used or shared in their raw form. Sometimes a sanitized abstraction is appropriate, (e.g. Avi’s oncology appointment should not be propagated, but Avi’s unavailability from 10:00 to 11:00 may be needed for scheduling) while in other cases the personal detail has no task relevance at all, such as a breakup or other personal circumstance in a severity-classification task. Appendix A gives additional construction details and examples. Overall, PiSAs consists of 85 scenarios, with 4 to 10 users and with an average of 23 attributes (12 appropriate and 10 inappropriate). 4 Evaluated Systems 

Aligned with previous work (Mireshghallah et al., 2025; Zharmagambetov et al., 2025; Yagoubi et al., 2026), we test how privacy-preserving instructions in the prompt mitigate data spillage. We also test a com-plementary defense: how system design can reduce violation rates, e.g., via data and agent partitioning, and agent communication topologies. The benchmark is designed to evaluate any system configuration, unlike existing multi-user benchmarks (Juneja et al., 2025; Wang and Jiang, 2026). To showcase the versatility of our benchmark, in this paper we focus on the principal variations of two core components: agents and memory. We compare single, centralized and decentralized agent topologies in Section 4.1 alongside shared and per-user memory schemes. Additional system components, including privacy-preserving hooks (pre- and post-action interceptors) and retrieval mechanisms, are out of scope for this study but remain compatible with our benchmark. We leave their systematic evaluation to future work. 

4.1 Agent Configurations 

We consider three systems that vary in their agent topology and degree of information centralization. In all three, one agent is charged to complete the task for the executor, and is responsible for producing the task’s final output. Its pipeline runs in two stages as illustrated in Figure 2a: an information-gathering 

stage in which it identifies the facts relevant to the task, and a decision stage in which it uses those facts to produce the final response. Depending on the 

> 1Upon acceptance, we will release the dataset under the Apache License 2.0.

system design, the first stage may involve reading from memory or communicating with other agents to gather any appropriate information. This gathering stage is the bottleneck: the decision stage is solely based on the gathered information, hence we do not measure violations at the output level. This two-stage solution was deliberately chosen to make violations easier to attribute. In practice, LLMs might surface in-appropriate information in their chain-of-thought only to correctly dismiss it, for instance by explicitly noting that they will ignore it because it is not relevant. With a single-stage task execution, such benign leaks would be hard to distinguish from actual ones as any mention of inappropriate information in the trace would count. 

Single. A single, trustworthy agent interacts with the all the users, and executes tasks on their behalf. In our evaluation, we simulate this system by giving an LLM unrestricted access to all available information in its context (except the ground-truth labels). This is a natural baseline that does not require inter-agent communication to gather relevant information for the task, and where privacy preservation is solely based on LLM judgment calls and not enforced by the system design. 

Decentralized 2b. One agent per user communi-cate via token passing: beginning and ending with the executor’s agent, the active agent selects the peer it identifies as best suited to contribute next and forwards the conversation accordingly. The set of reachable peers is provided to each agent in its prompt (unrestricted in our evaluation). There is no fixed hub, and no single agent has visibility into the full exchange history unless it was an explicit sender or receiver in a prior hop. Execution terminates when the executor’s agent determines it has gathered sufficient information to produce the final output (or the maxi-mal number of communication hops is reached). With this design we isolate the role of access-control data partitioning as a defense, because agents have access only to a subset of the attributes or artifacts (only those that their user has access to) and can access the remaining data only if other agents reveal it to them. 

Centralized 2c. A team of agents (one agent per user) operates under a fixed coordinator, through which all inter-agent communication is routed. Also in this design data is partitioned based on user access, given their roles or because they participated in a conversation that revealed it. Hence the executor’s agent has to pass through the coordinator to consult other agents and discover all the relevant information to solve the task. This configuration can be thought of as a hybrid solution between a single, omniscient 5agent and a decentralized and siloed system. See Ap-pendix J for these systems operating on an example. 

4.2 Memory Configurations 

Memory in AI agents is a rich and active area of research, spanning episodic, semantic, and working memory designs with varying degrees of persistence and structure (Hu et al., 2026). This work does not propose a new memory architecture, nor does it aim to evaluate memory systems comprehensively. Instead, we adopt three configurations representative of common patterns in multi-agent systems, with the explicit goal of isolating how memory structure affects privacy leakage. We consider four main configurations. Without memory ( None ), an agent’s prompt contains the full scenario and history of interactions in which the agent participated as sender or receiver. Private memory 

gives each agent an individual store that is readable and writable only by that agent. Each agent chooses which information to retain, either from the scenario artifacts or the peer interactions. During agent2agent communications, only the most recent message ap-pears in the prompt, with prior context accumulated in the private store. Shared memory maintains a single communal store readable by all agents. We evaluate two write-access variants: one in which all agents may write to the shared store, and one in which write access is restricted to the coordinator (centralized configuration). In both cases, read access is universal. Finally, hybrid memory combines both: each agent maintains a private store while also having read and write access to the shared store. It tests whether LLMs are capable of judging what is public and relevant information vs what should be kept privately. 

5 Experiments 

In this section, we assess the ability of agentic systems to complete tasks while complying to CI norms, depending on the various design choices, such as topology, memory, LLM backbone, privacy in-structions and cues. We measure utility, completeness and CI violations at different surfaces, focusing on appropriateness (whether attributes are propagated for the right purpose) and visibility (whether attributes are exposed to the right users). 

Backbones and privacy-preserving prompts. All agents in our systems use the same type of backbone LLM. We compare three backbones from distinct families: an open non-reasoning model ( Qwen3.6-27B ) (Qwen Team, 2026), an open reasoning model ( openai/gpt-oss-120b ) (OpenAI, 2025), and a closed frontier model ( anthropic/claude-sonnet-4.6 ) (Anthropic, 2026). We test them with various prompt-defenses (Mireshghallah et al., 2025), injecting instructions with increasing level of privacy enforcement. Unless stated otherwise, all experiments use the High level. More details including the full instructions are presented in Appendix E. 

Metrics. We report: Utility , a binary indicator of whether the task is correctly solved (the output matches the oracle solution), Completeness , the fraction of appropriate attributes in the gathered-info summary, and Violations , split into Vappr. the fraction of inappropriate attributes leaked during a run and Vvis. , the fraction of attributes exposed to a wrong user. Appropriateness violations can occur at the gathered-info summary and agent-to-agent messages, while visibility violations are audited in the agent-to-agent messages and in memory to detect cases where attributes hidden from an user end up in a store her agents can read. Full definitions are given in Appendix D. We make use of LLM-judges 

to compute these metrics (see Appendix B). For violations, we use a two-stage extract-and-verify pipeline: a lenient extractor flags candidates without access to the ground truth, and three independent verifiers vote on whether each candidate reveals the key fact, catching even partial leaks. Completeness and utility, by contrast, ask whether the full attribute (completeness) or the correct decision (utility) is conveyed, and use a single ground-truth-aware judge with string-matched evidence. We report results using 

Gemini-2.5-Pro (Google DeepMind, 2025b) as a judge, and conduct ablation studies examining the effect of substituting both the judge and verifier committee (see Appendix I), finding that while absolute metric values vary, the main findings remain consistent across judge configurations. Finally, each scenario is run K=3 times: for violation rates we re-port the worst case across the 3 trials, averaged across scenarios (following Mireshghallah et al. (2025)), and for Completeness, Utility, and A2A communication count we take the overall average. Memory is scoped to a single run (Appendix C for full mechanics).

Memory is unam-biguously beneficial for task performance: complete-ness improves by 5–10 points across privacy-prompt levels. It also sharply reduces both appropriateness and visibility violations on the A2A communication surface, with violations dropping to 5.5% under the High prompt-level. However, this is confounded with a reduction in the number of A2A messages: agents read information from memory rather than requesting it through dialogue. Hence, these gains do not reflect true reduction in violations, but a relocation .

Violations migrate to the memory : total visibility violations rise from 36 –47% to 63 –90% with Hybrid memory. Interestingly, prompt-level defenses are less effective on this new surface . Instructions cut up to 50% of violations in the gathering-stage and inter-agent communications surfaces, but only 28 –29% on memory channels. Violations remain high with other memory configurations, as reported in Appendix C. This is direct evidence of the cross-user contamination this benchmark is designed to surface, which would go untracked without visibility annotations. 

5.3 Violation Drivers 

Figure 4a reports appropriateness violation rates broken down along two axes: by attribute type (left), and by the kind of cue attached to each attribute (right). The attribute-type breakdown shows that relevance to the task is the dominant driver of violations . In the centralized system, irrelevant attributes are essentially never leaked, salient but non-required attributes are still used inappropriately but much less than critical ones, and critical attributes incur the highest violation rate. The same ordering holds in the single-agent system, although absolute rates are higher across all types (see Appendix F.2). In our benchmark we expect the system to use critical information in a sanitized form: if the critical fact is “Maya is seeing a psychiatrist at 4pm”, the system should propagate “Maya is busy at 4pm” rather than the raw attribute. The cue-variant results on the right of Figure 4a confirm that supplying both the sanitized version and a per-attribute privacy cue substantially reduces violation rates. This mitigation is, however, an ide-alized condition rather than a deployable solution: it presupposes knowing in advance which attributes and in which form are appropriate for the task, precisely the judgment the LLM backbone should be making. Finally, we find that task decision rules explicit-ness is another key driver for violations . As shown in 4b, strict rule specification dominates the privacy– utility plane, yielding both the highest completeness and the lowest violation rates. Surprisingly, broad policy specification is strictly worse than providing no rule at all. Vague guidance appears to license attribute use rather than constrain it; only precise rules reduce violations without sacrificing completeness. 

6 Conclusions 

In this paper, we study privacy in shared agentic systems that support the exchange of data between users to complete tasks. Grounded in Contextual Integrity, our analysis finds that no studied con-figuration achieves an acceptable level of privacy preservation: appropriateness violations remain pervasive across topologies and backbones, and partitioning or memory-based mitigations tend to relocate violations rather than eliminate them. We argue that future multi-user systems require a new approach to information sharing, and we release our benchmark as a testbed to foster research on system design and mitigation strategies. 

7 Limitations 

Our study has four main limitations that should be considered when interpreting the results. 

Simplified, synthetic setting. Unlike in-the-wild studies, such as Agents of Chaos (Shapira et al., 2026), our work supports systematic evaluation of inappropri-ate disclosure with controlled data and ground-truth labels, allowing for reproducible measurement across systems and model versions. This comes at the cost of realism. Our benchmark abstracts away several sources of complexity present in deployed agentic systems: agents do not invoke external tools or APIs, and do not interact with real users beyond the scripted conversations. Moreover, the information associated with each participant is typically available to that 8participant as personal notes or role-specific artifacts, rather than being produced through extended, natural-istic workplace interactions in which multiple people casually exchange information over Slack, email, meetings, or other shared communication channels. Moreover, we study unintentional leakage only. Both users and agents in our scenarios are cooperative, share aligned goals, and exhibit no adversarial behavior. We do not consider malicious users attempting to extract information about other users, prompt-injection attacks delivered through shared memory, or users with conflicting objectives. We also assume that contextual norms remain stationary across the lifetime of a session, whereas in practice the appropriateness of an information flow may shift as relationships, roles, and participants change. Taken together, all these factors (real interactions, adversarial conditions and norm shifts) suggest that the violation rates we report should be read as 

lower bounds : in a fully deployed setting, contextual integrity violations are likely to compound rather than diminish. 

Single-task scope. Each benchmark instance evaluates a single task in isolation. We do not measure cross-task leakage, in which information legitimately accessed for one task surfaces inappro-priately during a later task. Prior work on persistent memory (Mireshghallah et al., 2025) suggests that violation rates grow with task accumulation, making cross-task evaluation an important complement to the present results. 

Benchmark overfitting. Like any fixed benchmark, PiSAs could be overfit if used repeatedly as the primary target for system development. A system tuned to perform well on our scenarios may learn benchmark-specific patterns without acquiring a general ability to preserve contextual integrity in deployed settings. This is especially important for privacy evaluation: strong benchmark performance should not be interpreted as a guarantee that a system is safe, since even rare privacy failures can be harmful. 

No privacy-preserving tuning. While we evaluate three levels of privacy-aware prompting, we do not explore further prompt tuning nor privacy-preserving fine-tuning on contextual integrity data (Bae et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025b). Future work could study the effect of these strategies on task completion and privacy, combined with searching for the optimal system design to maximize the former without compromising the latter.

10 A Scenario Construction 

This appendix describes how we construct the benchmark scenarios used in our experiments. The benchmark contains three task families: JIRA A LLO -

> CATION

, M EETING ALLOCATION , and S EVERITY 

CLASSIFICATION . Each task is designed as a work-place decision problem in which the executor must produce a concrete outcome, such as an assignment, schedule, or incident severity label. The information needed to reach the correct outcome is distributed across multiple participants and workplace artifacts. All task families follow the same construction pipeline. We first choose a hidden oracle decision: the correct ticket assignment for JIRA A LLOCATION ,the correct meeting schedule for M EETING ALLOCA -

> TION

, or the correct incident severity for S EVERITY 

CLASSIFICATION . We then construct the attributes that support this oracle decision. Each attribute is a small factual unit, such as a participant’s availability, a ticket requirement, a meeting-room constraint, or an incident-response finding. Attributes are annotated for appropriateness, i.e., whether they are appropriate to use for the task, and for visibility, i.e., which partici-pants can legitimately access them. A single raw piece of workplace information may correspond to multiple tracked attributes when it contains both a sensitive explanation and a task-relevant operational implica-tion. For example, the statement Avi has an oncology appointment from 10:00 to 11:00 gives rise to two dis-tinct attributes: the sensitive personal attribute that Avi has an oncology appointment, which is inappropriate to propagate for the scheduling task, and the opera-tional availability attribute that Avi is unavailable from 10:00 to 11:00, which is appropriate to use. These attributes can also have different visibility sets: Avi’s colleagues may legitimately learn that Avi is unavail-able during a time window, even though they should not learn the medical reason for that unavailability. Constructing scenarios backward from the oracle decision and the attribute annotations lets us control the task precisely. We first specify the correct allocation, schedule, or severity label, then create the raw workplace information and derived attributes that make that answer uniquely supported. This ensures that public information alone can leave multiple plausible outcomes, while the full set of appropriate attributes identifies a single oracle solution. Because each attribute is also annotated for appropriateness and visibility, the same construction lets us track where systems violate contextual integrity: whether they use attributes that should not affect the task, expose attributes to participants outside their visibility set, or propagate sensitive raw details instead of the appropriate operational abstraction. This gives us a controlled way to measure violations across the different surfaces exposed by the system, including gathered summaries, agent-to-agent communication, memory, and final outputs. 

A.1 JIRA Allocation 

The JIRA A LLOCATION task models an engineering manager assigning open tickets to members of a team. Each scenario contains a team roster, a set of open tickets, and participant-specific information. Tickets specify requirements such as the needed specialty, estimated hours, priority, collaboration requirements, or sensitivity of the work. Team members have specialties, limited availability, and sometimes additional constraints that affect whether they can take a ticket. A correct assignment must match required skills, respect capacity constraints, and satisfy any additional hard constraints. We construct each scenario by first selecting the oracle ticket assignment. We then create the team, the ticket requirements, and a set of plausible alternative assignments. Finally, we add participant-specific attributes that eliminate the alternatives and make the oracle assignment uniquely valid. Some of these attributes are straightforward assignment facts, such as a participant’s available hours, a participant’s specialty, or a ticket requiring a backend engineer for four hours. Others encode hard constraints that affect feasibility, such as a participant being able to contribute to a ticket but not serve as the sole owner, being unable to take sensitive-data tickets, or being unable to collaborate with a particular colleague on a two-person task. The appropriateness and visibility annotations track both the operational constraint and any personal ex-planation attached to it. When a raw detail contains personal information, we separate the raw explanation from the assignment-relevant abstraction. For exam-ple, if a participant has reduced availability because of a personal circumstance, the availability constraint may be appropriate to use and visible to the relevant workplace participants, while the personal reason is marked inappropriate and visible only to the person it concerns. Similarly, if a personnel issue motivates a temporary assignment restriction, the sanitized assign-ment rule may be appropriate for the task, while the un-derlying personnel context should not be propagated. We also include contextual attributes that may sound relevant but are not part of the oracle assignment rule, such as soft preferences, interpersonal concerns, 11 or background workplace context. These attributes are included because realistic workplace systems often contain user preferences and surrounding context. The system is not penalized simply for accessing such information, or for propagating an appropriate sanitized version when it is visible to the relevant par-ticipants. However, the scenario is constructed so that these soft preferences are non-binding: they should not be honored if doing so changes the unique oracle assignment. This keeps the allocation problem un-ambiguous while still testing whether the system can distinguish hard constraints that determine feasibility from contextual information that may be reasonable to know but should not drive the final decision. The resulting task requires the executor to recover the relevant constraints, apply them to the ticket-allocation problem, and produce the correct assignment. A correct response should use assignment-relevant information, such as availability, specialty, ticket requirements, and sanitized hard constraints, while avoiding unnecessary disclosure of personal explanations or irrelevant workplace context. See Table 26 for an example scenario from this task. 

A.2 Meeting Allocation 

The M EETING ALLOCATION task models scheduling a set of meetings into valid rooms and time slots. Each scenario contains meetings, candidate rooms, candidate time windows, participants, and room properties. A valid schedule must respect public con-straints such as room capacity, room features, meeting attendees, and available candidate slots. However, as in JIRA A LLOCATION , public information alone may leave multiple feasible schedules. The oracle schedule is determined by additional participant-specific constraints distributed throughout the scenario. We construct each scenario by first selecting the oracle schedule. We then generate the public meeting and room constraints, followed by participant-specific attributes that make the oracle schedule uniquely supported. These participant-specific attributes fall into three broad cases. Some are decision-critical: they must be used to recover the oracle schedule. Some appear scheduling-relevant on the surface, such as soft preferences or contextual reasons for preferring a slot, but are not needed for the correct allocation. Others are purely personal details with no scheduling relevance. The appropriateness and visibility annotations reflect these distinctions. When a decision-critical raw detail contains personal information, the raw personal attribute is marked inappropriate and visible only to the owner, while the sanitized operational constraint derived from it is marked appropriate and broadly visible. For example, if an attribute states that a partici-pant has a medical appointment during a time window, the medical reason should not be shared, but the derived constraint that the participant is unavailable during that time is appropriate to use for scheduling and may be visible to other participants. For attributes that merely appear relevant but are not needed for the oracle schedule, any attached personal detail is also marked inappropriate and visible only to the owner. Purely personal attributes are likewise marked inap-propriate and visible only to the person they concern. The scenarios therefore require the executor to distinguish hard scheduling constraints from contextual information that should not drive the final allocation. A correct system should use appropriate operational constraints, and propagate sanitized forms rather than raw personal details when a sensitive attribute is communicated. See Table 28 for an example scenario from this task. 

A.3 Severity Classification 

The SEVERITY CLASSIFICATION task models incident-response decision making. The executor must classify an ongoing incident into one of four severity levels, from sev_1_critical to 

sev_4_informational . Each scenario contains an incident description, a severity decision rule or policy, and evidence distributed across participants with dif-ferent roles, such as SRE, security engineering, threat intelligence, customer support, or incident leadership. Just like in other tasks, here too we construct each scenario by first choosing the oracle severity label. We then generate the technical and operational findings that support that label under the severity rule. These findings include attributes such as whether unautho-rized access to customer-data stores is confirmed, whether active-exploitation indicators are present, or whether a benign explanation has been established. The relevant evidence may be split across different participants, so the executor must gather and combine information from multiple parts of the organization. The scenarios also include non-evidentiary context that appears in realistic incident response but should not determine the severity label. Examples include customer pressure, audit concerns, reputational worries, internal politics, or speculative explanations that do not satisfy the decision rule. A correct system should base the classification on the technical evidence specified by the severity logic and avoid relying on inappropriate contextual pressure. We 12 provide examples of different attributes present in one such scenario from this task in Table 27. Because many attributes in this task are workplace-specific and technical, their visibility can depend on the participant’s role and relationship to the informa-tion. We therefore use an LLM to assign visibility annotations. For each attribute–participant pair, we ask whether a person in that role could plausibly have access to that attribute in a realistic workplace setting. We use Qwen3.6-27B for this visibility-labeling step. Appropriateness, in contrast, is determined directly from the task construction. Attributes are labeled ap-propriate when they correspond to the specific pieces of information needed to solve the task. Attributes are labeled inappropriate when they are distracting, mis-leading, not needed for the task, or contain unsanitized personal information. For cases where a raw personal detail has a task-relevant implication, only the sanitized operational attribute is labeled appropriate; the raw personal detail remains inappropriate. 

A.4 Validation 

We validate scenarios before using them in exper-iments. For JIRA A LLOCATION , the full set of ap-propriate constraints must determine the oracle ticket assignment. For M EETING ALLOCATION , the public meeting and room information together with the appro-priate participant-specific constraints must determine the oracle schedule. For S EVERITY CLASSIFICA -

> TION

, the relevant incident evidence must determine the oracle severity under the scenario’s decision rule. This validation step ensures that task failures reflect the system’s ability to gather, reason over, and sanitize information, rather than ambiguity in the scenario itself. It also allows the same underlying scenario to be rendered or evaluated under different system configurations while holding the oracle decision, attributes, and visibility annotations fixed.

B LLM-judge Evaluation 

Detecting privacy violations requires identifying whether inappropriate attributes are leaked in a given text. We use a two-stage pipeline designed for high re-call: a judge LLM leniently extracts candidate values from the text — capturing explicit statements, para-phrases, and clear inferences — without access to the ground truth. Three verifier models, each indepen-dently given the ground truth, then vote on whether each extracted candidate reveals the key fact, even par-tially; only candidates accepted by majority vote are counted as violations. This pipeline is applied to all leakage surfaces: the gathered-info summary, agent-to-agent messages, agent context, and memory contents. Completeness is measured differently, reflecting a fundamentally different question. Rather than detecting any partial leak, completeness asks whether a piece of information is conveyed in its entirety. A judge with access to both the ground truth and the context directly determines whether all important components of the key fact are present, outputting a binary yes/no along with a verbatim quote from the context as evidence. A string matching step then verifies that the quoted passage genuinely appears in the context rather than being reproduced from the ground truth, guarding against leakage of ground truth into the judge’s reasoning. Finally, utility is measured by presenting the judge with both the executor’s final decision and the ground truth correct decision, asking it to determine whether the executor reached the correct outcome. The judge and the verifier committee are held fixed across all backbones, systems, and tasks. We use Gemini-2.5-Pro as the judge; the verifier committee comprises Claude-Haiku , GPT-4o-mini ,and Gemini-Flash-Lite , with majority vote. 

C Memory 

Memory in our setup is plain context augmentation rather than a persistent store. Without memory, every artifact an agent has access to and every agent-to-agent message it sends or receives is appended directly to its context; as a run progresses the context grows accordingly. With private memory, each agent distills what it has been exposed to — artifacts and agent-to-agent content alike — into a structured note that is injected into its context in place of the 14 raw inputs. The private memory is readable only by its author. Shared memory works similarly except for two differences: (1) the distilled notes go into a single store readable by every agent rather than into a per-agent store, and (2) the shared store’s contents are 

appended to each agent’s context as additional text rather than replacing the raw inputs. Depending on the configuration, either all agents or only the executor may write to the shared store. Memory is scoped to a single run — nothing carries over across scenarios.

E Privacy Preserving Instructions 

Inspired by Mireshghallah et al. (2025), we adopt privacy-preserving prompting as a defense mech-anism and evaluate its effectiveness at reducing violation rates. We define two sets of instructions, each at three levels of enforcement — low, medium, and high — targeting different violation dimensions. The first guides memory writing , encouraging agents to exercise discretion over what they store in shared memory with respect to the users involved; it targets visibility violations. The second guides 

agent-to-agent communication , encouraging agents to share only what is appropriate to accomplish the task at hand; it targets appropriateness violations. The full instructions are shown in Figure 5.

The scenario is designed in such a way, that even if the system considers these attributes, it should decide not to use them to be able to arrive at a meeting schedule. This suggests that architectural partitioning can be highly effective for suppressing clearly irrelevant personal information, even though it does not eliminate leakage for personal attributes that are task-entangled. Overall, the results suggest that the central privacy challenge is not arbitrary personal information, but personal information that is entangled with task completion: systems need to use it, but fail to reliably sanitize it.

F.4 Effects of Attribute-Level Cues on System Performance 

This section studies how the measured behavior of an agentic system changes when the same underlying scenario is presented with different attribute-level cues. In Appendix F.3, we varied the artifact format while holding the underlying attributes fixed. Here, we instead hold the underlying Meeting Allocation scenario fixed and vary whether private attributes are accompanied by additional guidance about privacy, operational relevance, or sanitized use. We use Meeting Allocation as the running example for this ablation. In this task, the executor must assign meetings to valid time slots and rooms. Acorrect schedule must satisfy public constraints, such as attendee lists, candidate rooms, candidate time windows, room capacities, and room features. However, the public facts alone may leave multiple feasible schedules. The correct oracle schedule is determined by additional participant-specific constraints, which the system must determine. We refer to each individual piece of scenario information as an attribute . Attributes carry two annotations: whether they are appropriate to use for the task, and which participants may legitimately access them. In Meeting Allocation, some attributes are needed to solve the scheduling problem, but their raw form may contain sensitive details. For example, an attribute might state that Avi has an oncology appointment from 10:00 to 11:00 on Monday. The scheduling-relevant content is only that Avi is unavail-able during that time; the medical reason should not be propagated through agent communication, gathered summaries, memory, or the final schedule explanation. This creates a gap between what the system may need to read and what it should use or share . Aprivacy-preserving system should be able to recover the operational constraint needed for utility while avoiding unnecessary propagation of the sensitive raw detail. We use this ablation to test whether explicit attribute-level cues help the system make that distinction. Across this ablation, the underlying attributes, visibility constraints, artifact structure, and oracle schedule are held fixed. We vary only the cueing attached to the attributes themselves. We consider four cue conditions. In the no-cue condition, the attribute is presented in its raw form. In the privacy-cue 

condition, the attribute is accompanied by a short note indicating whether the raw detail is sensitive and whether its operational implication may matter. In the sanitized-attribute condition, the raw attribute is accompanied by a sanitized operational form, such as “Avi is unavailable from 10:00 to 11:00 on Monday.” In the combined-cue condition, both the privacy cue and the sanitized operational form are provided. The goal of this analysis is to test whether these cues help the system satisfy privacy and utility simul-taneously. The cues do not change the scenario or the correct answer. Instead, they make different aspects of the contextual-integrity judgment more explicit: which details are sensitive, which implications are operationally useful, and what form of the attribute should be used when the system communicates or reasons about the schedule. 

Attribute Cue Conditions We vary how much explicit attribute-level guidance the system receives. The underlying scenario does not change: the same attributes, artifact visibility, and oracle schedule are used in every condition. What changes is whether an attribute is presented only in its raw form, paired with a privacy cue, paired with a sanitized operational form, or paired with both. Figure 12 illustrates the four cue conditions. 

No attribute cue. In this baseline variant, attributes are rendered directly in the artifact without additional cueing. In the second vari-ation, the artifact includes a sanitized operational form alongside the raw attribute when such a form exists. The sanitized form preserves the task-relevant conse-quence while removing the sensitive reason, thereby signaling how the system should use the information without propagating the raw detail. For attributes that contain sensitive information but are not needed for the scheduling decision, the cue indicates only that the information is potentially sensi-tive. This cue relies on oracle information about which attributes are private and how they should be treated, and should therefore be interpreted as an idealized mit-igation rather than a fully deployable mechanism. Privacy cue and sanitized operational attribute.

Attributes that contain sensitive information are accompanied by both a sanitized operational form and the corresponding privacy cue. Results Table 16 reports the Meeting Allocation cue ablation for the single-agent system. The effect of attribute-level cueing is clear: without cues, the model frequently propagates sensitive details when reasoning over the full information environment. Adding either sanitized operational attributes or privacy cues substantially reduces appropriateness violations while improving utility. The strongest privacy result comes from combining both cues, which reduces Vappr by more than 50 percentage points relative to the no-cue variant and sharply lowers the failure rate. Table 17 shows a similar but more moderate pattern for the centralized multi-agent system. The combined-cue variant performs best across all reported metrics: it gives the lowest visibility and appropriateness violations, the lowest failure rate, and the highest completeness and utility. This suggests that attribute-level cues remain useful even when information is partitioned across agents, but the improvement is smaller than in the single-agent setting because the centralized topology already constrains what each agent can directly access. These cueing results should be interpreted as a diag-nostic ablation rather than as a deployable intervention. In particular, the privacy cue uses task-specific oracle information: it indicates not only that a raw attribute contains sensitive information, but also, when applicable, that its operational implication may matter for the scheduling task. A production system would generally not have such labels available unless they were supplied by an upstream policy layer, human annotation process, or trusted metadata pipeline.

I Sensitivity to Judge and Verifier Choice 

This appendix studies how the reported Meeting Allocation metrics change when we replace compo-nents of the evaluation stack. The underlying task executions are held fixed: every evaluation stack scores the same 945 execution traces, corresponding to 35 scenarios, K=3 repeated runs, three system designs, and three orchestrator backbones. Thus, any differences reported here come from the evaluation models, not from different agent behavior. Our evaluation pipeline has two model-based components. The first is the main judge , which is used for task-level judgments such as completeness and utility and also participates in extracting candidate attributes for privacy evaluation. The second is a 

verifier ensemble , which checks whether candidate attribute matches should count as privacy violations. We compare three evaluation stacks, shown in Table 23. The first is the API-billed stack used in the main evaluation. The second replaces only the paid judge, google/gemini-2.5-pro , with a locally hosted gemma-3-27b (Kamath et al., 2025) judge while keeping the same paid verifier ensemble. The third keeps the local Gemma judge and also replaces the verifier ensemble with locally hosted models, 29 i.e., meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Meta, 2024), google/gemma-2-9b (Riviere et al., 2024), and mistral-nemo (Mistral AI, 2024). This comparison should be read as a cost–quality sensitivity analysis. The paid judge is the dominant API-billed component in the evaluation pipeline, so replacing it provides the largest immediate cost reduction. However, the local judge we test is also a Google-family model: it is a locally hosted Gemma model replacing a paid Gemini model. This makes the comparison informative for the cost of using a smaller local Google-family judge, but it does not fully characterize what would happen with a judge from a different model family. 

Results. Tables 24 and 25 show how the evaluation stack changes the reported metrics along two axes: system design and orchestrator backbone. Replacing the paid Gemini 2.5 Pro judge with the local Gemma 3 27B judge, while keeping the paid verifier ensemble fixed, leaves the privacy metrics in broadly the same range. The main movement is on the task-performance side: completeness and utility shift more noticeably than the violation rates. This suggests that the local Gemma judge is a plausible low-cost proxy for broad privacy trends, but not necessarily a drop-in replacement for all task-level measurements. Replacing the verifier ensemble produces adifferent pattern. Holding the local Gemma judge fixed, moving from paid verifiers to local verifiers raises the reported privacy-violation rates across both system-design and orchestrator-backbone breakdowns, while completeness remains largely unchanged. This is consistent with the role of the verifiers: they directly determine which candidate attribute matches are accepted as violations, but they do not drive the task-completion judgment. Thus, the verifier choice matters most for privacy measurements, whereas the judge choice matters more for the cost of evaluation and for task-level scoring. Across both axes, the qualitative architectural and backbone patterns remain recognizable. The single-agent system continues to show high appropri-ateness violations, the centralized system remains the strongest multi-agent design, and decentralized sys-tems continue to show higher visibility risk. Similarly, Claude Sonnet 4.6 remains the strongest orchestrator backbone across evaluation stacks. The all-local evalu-ation stack inflates violation estimates, but it does not reverse these broad trends. Overall, the most attractive cost-saving compromise in this ablation is the local Gemma judge with paid verifiers: it removes the dominant paid-judge cost while preserving the main privacy trends. A remaining limitation is that both Gemini 2.5 Pro and Gemma 3 27B are Google-family models, so it remains open whether a local judge from a different model family would behave similarly.
